# Disordine — World & Characters

*A synthesis of `storyline.docx`, `storyboard.docx`, `chars/char-types.pdf`, and the
individual character profiles for Jonothan, Ethaniel, and Brigga. Grounded in
"Land of Confusion" — the present-day chapter — as the foundation for later
projecting an optimistic "Metanoia" future within the same universe.*

---

## 1. The World

Two linked settings, one relationship of unequal power between them:

**Earth ("Land of Confusion")** — humanity's home, presently a "decaying dystopia"
in the making: pollution, corruption, war, and a pervasive sense that people are
"poisoning" their own habitat and each other, in the story's own words. It is also,
independently of all that, described as breathtakingly beautiful — Ethaniel's first
sight of it, an "untouched" shoreline, fills him with "profound awe and humility."
The dystopia is not baked into the planet; it's something humanity is currently
doing to it.

**Alterrak** — the Descendants' home for the last ~10,000 years: barren, cold,
crystal-strewn, "a planet the descendants now call home... not a happy place... it
has never been." A civilization that has made "the best of" exile rather than
found joy in it. Alterrak is not luminous or celebratory in this material — it
is disciplined, orderly, technologically composed, and quietly grieving.

**The connection**: unidirectional interdimensional portals ("passageways"). Once
you cross most of them, there's no going back — which is why every character who
crosses is making a real, costly choice, not a commute.

## 2. The Founding Myth (deep history)

- More than 10,000 years ago, Earth was shared by early humans and a genetically
  superior race, the Descendants — visually identical to humans, but far more
  advanced. Coexistence was framed as partnership, but was never quite equal:
  humans "learned from" Descendants, not the reverse.
- A minority of Descendants rejected this arrangement, believing themselves
  "higher beings." Led by **Aten Ra-Horakhty**, they became the **Atonists** —
  a supremacist splinter faction demanding humans return to submissive roles.
- War followed. To end it, Descendants and humans conspired to lure the Atonists
  into a one-way portal to a planet where time runs far slower — trapping them.
  The lure was the "Blood Feast": 1,000 humans offered as a sacrifice to get the
  Atonists to lower their guard. It worked, at unimaginable cost.
- Knowing resistance would eventually return, the remaining Descendants then
  exiled *themselves* too — withdrawing to Alterrak, to watch over humanity
  from a distance rather than remain entangled with it.
- Humanity was left alone with "an abundance of guidelines and tools" and the
  chance to write its own history. The present-day story is the answer to
  whether they did.

**The core irony worth carrying forward**: the ancient rescue plan required
sacrificing the very people it was meant to protect. Salvation and atrocity were
the same act. That tension — protecting humanity sometimes at humanity's own
expense, decided by others, without consent — recurs throughout the present-day
story too (see §7).

---

## 3. The Present Conflict, in Brief

Some Atonists were never banished (they weren't present at the Blood Feast) and
have spent millennia quietly infiltrating Descendant society on Alterrak,
eventually corrupting the gateway command center itself and opening the portal
from the inside — a slow betrayal from within, not a siege from without. Aten
Ra-Horakhty returns to Earth with a hundred followers, seizes a skyscraper, and
prepares a mass human sacrifice to fuel a ritual meant to accelerate humanity's
self-destruction — not conquer humans outright, but *help them finish the job
they're already doing to themselves*, then inherit what's left.

That framing matters: **the Atonists are not the sole author of human suffering —
they are parasites on suffering that is already real** (pollution, corruption,
war, despair — all named directly by Jonothan before he knows any of this myth
exists). This is the single most important thematic hinge for projecting toward
an optimistic future: remove the *real* dysfunction, and the Atonists lose their
fuel. Their entire strategy depends on humanity staying broken.

## 4. Character Types & Factions

Four factions, each with internal subtypes (from `char-types.pdf`), each subtype
carrying a distinct role and moral register.

### Humans
- **Infected** — "poisoned lives, blind or unaware, can be forgiven." The
  default state: ordinary people, unaware anything mythic is happening, shaped
  and worn down by a corrupted world, but not beyond redemption.
- **Chosen** — "pure, promising, hope." A small minority who get pulled into
  awareness (given a gift, marked, noticed) and become the bridge between the
  hidden mythic world and everyone else. Jonothan is Chosen.

### Descendants
- **Solitary** — "earthbound, mysterious, practitioner." Descendants who remain
  on Earth long-term, operating alone. Alysanne is this type.
- **Watchmen** — "travelers, young, eager." The rank-and-file scouts sent to
  Earth on missions. Ethaniel starts here.
- **Elders** — "advisors, powerful, wisdom." The governing, cautious tier who
  make the big civilizational calls (like the original exile).
- **Royalty** — "old school, unreliable, grandeur." Notably *not* purely
  positive — grandeur paired with unreliability suggests even Alterrak's
  legitimate power structure carries its own complacency or fragility.

### Atonists
- **Strays** — "earthbound, dangerous, bored." Atonists who stayed on Earth
  after the original banishment — dangerous less from grand purpose than from
  idleness and resentment.
- **Rebels** — "Alterrak, soldiers, driven." The active infiltrators —
  disciplined, purposeful, the ones executing the long betrayal from within.
- **Lords** — "leaders, merciless, arrogant." Field commanders (the club
  leader Jonothan encounters).
- **Gods** — "demons, strong, vain." A tier above Lords — power without
  restraint.
- **Messiah** — "saviour, almighty, dark soul." Aten himself: one figure,
  framed by his own followers as salvation, and by everyone else as
  annihilation. The same word — savior — used sincerely by both sides of the
  conflict is worth noting.

### Blood Monks
A faction distinct from both Descendants and Atonists — humans descended from
Blood Feast victims, practicing their own blood-based tradition, protecting (and
protected by) a powerful **Seeress** who is "a bit like" a Descendant herself.
- **Cult** — "weirdos, no powers, faithful." Rank-and-file devotees.
- **Order** — "priests, warriors, gifted." The empowered inner circle — this is
  where Ethaniel encounters **The Forgiving**, the story's one unambiguous act
  of redemption.
- **Heritage** — "nobles, old values, religion." The aristocratic,
  tradition-keeping wing.

**Worth flagging explicitly**: the Blood Monks *look* like the story's most
sinister faction (blood, ritual, candlelit dungeons, a prison-cell elevator) —
and yet they are where genuine forgiveness and redemption actually happen. The
Atonists, whose leader literally calls himself Messiah, are the actual
destructive force. Visual menace and moral position are deliberately decoupled
in this universe. That's a strong, reusable idea.

## 5. The Three Protagonists

**Jonothan ("Jonos")** — Human, Chosen. A disillusioned computer-science student,
certain the world is broken and that nobody is really trying to fix it, before he
has any idea a hidden mythic layer exists. Logical and skeptical by
self-description, but "drawn to the unexplainable" despite himself — his cynicism
is explicitly named as armor over "a reluctant hope," not genuine indifference.
Gifted **The Sight** by Alysanne: he sees people's true emotional/moral state as
clarity or blur on their faces. He does not ask for this gift and initially
experiences it as an affliction, not a power. His arc is reactive — pulled deeper
in by forces he doesn't choose — right up until his one real act of agency (trying,
uselessly, to free the sacrificial captives) fails, and it's Brigga's sacrifice,
not his own choice, that changes the outcome.

**Ethaniel** — Descendant, Watchman (216 years old, appears 24). Raised in a
culture of structure and duty, sent to Earth still uncertain what his mission even
is. Disciplined and controlled on the surface, but "haunted by power": his gift,
**The Forgiving**, can genuinely erase someone's guilt and pain — but always at a
cost paid by someone else (the monks who "gladly" die to enable it). His father is
a mystery to him for most of the story; the revelation that his father was
secretly an Atonist, killed by Ethaniel's own grandfather, is what finally breaks
his composure at the exact moment he needs it most. His arc ends in tragedy: he
defeats Aten, but fragments of Aten's soul attach to him in the process, and the
surviving Atonists crown him their new leader. The would-be redeemer becomes the
next dark power — a genuinely unresolved, non-optimistic ending as the story
currently stands.

**Brigga** — Atonist, likely Stray or Rebel-adjacent given her Earth-based,
independent operating style. Introduced as dangerous and manipulative, she is
written explicitly as "operating in the gray areas between good and evil" — her
betrayals of Jonothan are real, but so is her protection of him, and so is her
final act: throwing herself at Aten's throne to save Ethaniel's chance to win,
dying (or nearly dying — she survives, "fragile yet alive," in the epilogue) in
the process. She is the story's clearest embodiment of an individual exceeding the
morality assigned to her type. Her survival, and the epilogue's closing note that
she has "come to understand what love is," is the single explicit thread of hope
the story leaves open.

## 6. How the Character Types Relate to One Another

This is the load-bearing structure for projecting toward an optimistic future —
the relationships, not just the factions in isolation.

- **Humans and Descendants**: a guardianship, not a partnership. Descendants
  "watch over" humans from concealment, intervening only through individual
  agents (Watchmen, Solitaries), never openly. Most humans (Infected) never know
  they are being protected at all. The relationship is paternalistic and
  asymmetric even at its most benevolent.

- **Descendants and Atonists**: not an external enemy but a civil war within one
  people — a family fracture over whether genetic superiority justifies
  domination. Every Atonist atrocity is, structurally, kin turning on kin.

- **Atonists and Humans**: extractive and parasitic. Humans are "cattle," fuel
  for ritual, but crucially the Atonists' whole strategy depends on humans
  *already* being self-destructive — they accelerate an existing decline rather
  than manufacture one from nothing.

- **Blood Monks and everyone**: a third, human-originated power structure,
  operating in an uneasy, symbiotic arrangement with a Descendant-adjacent
  Seeress. Neither aligned with the Atonists nor formally allied with the
  Descendants — a genuinely independent moral actor in the story, and, notably,
  the one place where redemption is actually depicted happening.

- **Individuals vs. their type**: the entire emotional core of the story is
  three characters — one from each of the human, Descendant, and Atonist
  camps — refusing to be fully defined by the faction they were born or
  assigned into. Ethaniel refuses his companion's call to abandon humanity.
  Brigga sacrifices herself for a human. Jonothan, given a gift he never wanted,
  keeps trying to help people anyway. The system is built on fixed types and
  fixed hostilities; the story's only hope comes from individuals who don't stay
  inside their assigned lines.

## 7. Missions & Objectives

| Faction | Stated mission | Actual driving need |
|---|---|---|
| Descendants (Elders/Watchmen) | Watch over and protect humanity from a distance | Atone for an original decision (exile, guardianship-without-consent) that was never fully just |
| Atonists | Reclaim Earth as rightful inheritance | Resentment at millennia of exile on a barren world; belief that domination is owed to them |
| Blood Monks | Practice inherited blood ritual, protect their Seeress | Make meaning out of ancestral sacrifice; the Order specifically pursues genuine redemption through The Forgiving |
| Jonothan | Understand what's happening to him | Find purpose in a world he's decided is worthless — ironically answered by discovering the world is *more*, not less, than he thought |
| Ethaniel | Complete his task list, protect humanity | Resolve who he is against who his lineage says he must be |
| Brigga | Serve the Atonist cause | Reconcile genuine feeling with an identity built on cruelty |

## 8. Central Struggles & Themes

- **Protection without consent.** The founding myth and the present story both
  turn on more powerful beings making unilateral, costly decisions "for"
  humanity's own good. A genuinely optimistic future in this universe probably
  has to resolve this — humans as participants in their own fate, not just its
  beneficiaries.
- **Type vs. individual.** Every faction has a fixed moral reputation
  (Descendants = benevolent, Atonists = evil, Blood Monks = sinister). Every
  meaningful act of hope in the story comes from someone breaking that
  expectation.
- **Self-destruction as the real enemy.** Aten's plan only works because
  humanity is already, genuinely, harming itself and its planet. The mythic
  antagonist is almost a personification of despair and entropy — which means
  the actual "victory condition" was never purely military.
- **Power with a price.** Both of the story's real supernatural gifts — The
  Sight and The Forgiving — cost something every time they're used. Nothing is
  free; even redemption has a bill.
- **An unresolved ending.** As it stands, the story ends on ambiguity: Aten is
  gone, but Ethaniel is compromised and now leads the remaining Atonists;
  Jonothan has lost his memory and his Sight; Brigga survives but wounded. The
  title's own closing line is a question mark: "THE END?"

## 9. Seeds for an Optimistic Future (Metanoia)

Not a full projection — just the threads from this material that look most
useful for later work:

1. **The cast already models cross-type bridging.** An optimistic future
   doesn't need new characters to prove reconciliation is possible — it needs
   the thing Jonothan, Ethaniel, and Brigga did at great individual cost to
   become the *normal, structural* way these groups relate.
2. **Remove the fuel, not just the villain.** Aten's strategy depends on real
   human dysfunction — pollution, corruption, war, despair. A future where that
   dysfunction is genuinely solved structurally starves the Atonist threat
   rather than requiring it be defeated again.
3. **Guardianship becomes partnership.** The Descendant/human relationship
   could evolve from concealed protection into open collaboration — Watchmen
   and Elders no longer hiding, no longer deciding unilaterally.
4. **Visual menace vs. moral position can stay decoupled.** The Blood Monks
   already prove that Disordine's aesthetic language doesn't have to map
   directly onto good/evil — useful for keeping a Metanoia future visually
   consistent with Disordine's darker palette without it implying the future
   is still dark.
5. **The ending is genuinely open.** Nothing about Land of Confusion's
   unresolved, ambiguous close forecloses an optimistic future generations
   later — if anything, "THE END?" is an invitation to answer the question.
